Week 1 – Atomic Structure & Electronic Configuration

Paper 1 | Topic 8 (Combined) | Both tiers

⚛️ Key spec facts (all students):
• All substances are made of atoms. Atoms of each element have a unique chemical symbol.
• An atom has a nucleus containing protons (+1) and neutrons (0), surrounded by electrons (−1) in shells (energy levels).
• Atomic number = number of protons. In a neutral atom, number of electrons = number of protons.
• Mass number = protons + neutrons. Number of neutrons = mass number − atomic number.
• Electrons occupy the lowest available energy levels first. Shell 1 holds max 2, shell 2 holds max 8, shell 3 holds max 8.
• Elements in the same group have the same number of electrons in their outer shell → similar chemical properties.
• Isotopes are atoms of the same element with the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons.

Click each card to flip it!

What charge does Na form, and why?
Na⁺ — loses 1 electron from outer shell (2,8,1 → 2,8). Metal atoms lose electrons to form positive ions.
What charge does Cl form, and why?
Cl⁻ — gains 1 electron to fill outer shell (2,8,7 → 2,8,8). Non-metal atoms gain electrons to form negative ions.
What charge does Ca form, and why?
Ca²⁺ — loses 2 electrons from outer shell (2,8,8,2 → 2,8,8). Group 2 metals form 2+ ions.
What charge does O form, and why?
O²⁻ — gains 2 electrons to fill outer shell (2,6 → 2,8). Group 6 non-metals form 2− ions.
⚠️ Examiner tip: The charge on an ion from a Group 1 or 2 metal = group number (e.g. Group 2 → 2+). The charge on an ion from a Group 6 or 7 non-metal = 8 − group number (e.g. Group 6 → 2−, Group 7 → 1−).

📝 Extra Exam-style Questions

(2 marks)(a) Chlorine has two main isotopes: Cl-35 and Cl-37. Explain what is meant by the term isotope using chlorine as an example.

✅ Isotopes are atoms of the same element with the same number of protons ✓ but different numbers of neutrons ✓. Cl-35 has 18 neutrons and Cl-37 has 20 neutrons, but both have 17 protons.

(2 marks)(b) An atom of aluminium has an atomic number of 13 and a mass number of 27. Determine its electronic configuration and the number of neutrons.

✅ Electronic configuration: 2, 8, 3 ✓
Neutrons = 27 − 13 = 14 neutrons ✓

(2 marks)(c) Potassium (K) is in Group 1 and sodium (Na) is also in Group 1. Explain why they have similar chemical properties.

✅ They are in the same group ✓ so they have the same number of electrons in their outer shell (both have 1 outer electron) ✓. The number of electrons in the outer shell determines chemical properties.

Week 2 – Ionic & Covalent Bonding

Paper 1 | Topic 9 (Combined) | Both tiers

🔗 Key spec facts:
Ionic bonding: occurs between a metal and a non-metal. Metal atoms lose electrons → positive ions. Non-metal atoms gain electrons → negative ions. Ions are held together by strong electrostatic forces of attraction between oppositely charged ions in all directions — this is ionic bonding. Ionic compounds have giant ionic lattice structures with high melting and boiling points. When melted or dissolved in water, they conduct electricity because ions are free to move.

Covalent bonding: occurs between non-metal atoms that share pairs of electrons. These bonds are strong. Small covalently bonded molecules (e.g. H₂O, CO₂) have weak intermolecular forces so have low melting/boiling points and do not conduct electricity.

📝 Ionic vs Covalent – MCQ practice

(1 mark)(a) Sodium chloride (NaCl) is an ionic compound. Which statement best describes why it has a high melting point?

It has strong covalent bonds between atoms
There are strong electrostatic forces of attraction between oppositely charged ions in all directions
It has a simple molecular structure with weak intermolecular forces

(1 mark)(b) Why can molten sodium chloride conduct electricity but solid sodium chloride cannot?

✅ In the solid the ions are held in fixed positions in the lattice and cannot move ✓. When molten, the ions are free to move ✓ and so can carry electrical charge through the liquid.

(1 mark)(c) Water (H₂O) has a much lower boiling point than sodium chloride. Explain why.

✅ Water consists of small molecules with weak intermolecular forces between them ✓. Only these weak intermolecular forces need to be overcome to boil water (not the strong covalent bonds) ✓. Sodium chloride has strong electrostatic forces between ions which require much more energy to break.

🃏 Bonding Flashcards

Click to flip!

Ionic bonding — metal + non-metal. Strong electrostatic forces between Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions in a giant ionic lattice.

📝 Extra Exam-style Questions

(2 marks)(d) Diamond and graphite are both forms of carbon. Diamond does not conduct electricity but graphite does. Explain why graphite conducts electricity.

✅ In graphite, each carbon atom only bonds to three other carbon atoms ✓, leaving one electron per atom delocalised / free to move ✓. These delocalised electrons can carry electrical charge through the structure.

(3 marks)(e) A student has two unknown white solids, X and Y. Solid X has a melting point of 801°C and conducts electricity when dissolved in water. Solid Y has a melting point of 43°C and does not conduct electricity. Identify the type of structure and bonding for each solid.

✅ Solid X: giant ionic lattice / ionic bonding ✓ — high melting point because of strong electrostatic forces; conducts when dissolved because ions are free to move.
Solid Y: simple (covalent) molecular structure ✓ — low melting point because intermolecular forces are weak ✓. Does not conduct because there are no free ions or electrons.

Week 3 – Balancing Symbol Equations

Paper 1 | Topic 8 (Combined) | Both tiers

⚖️ Key rule (law of conservation of mass): No atoms are lost or made during a chemical reaction — the mass of products equals the mass of reactants. Equations must be balanced: same number of atoms of each element on both sides. You can only change the large numbers (coefficients) in front of formulae — never change the small subscript numbers inside a formula.

Week 4 – Quantitative Chemistry

Paper 1 | Topic 10 (Combined) | Mr = both tiers | Moles = HT only

📐 Relative formula mass (Mr) = sum of all the atomic masses in the formula  FT & HT

HT only  Number of moles = mass (g) ÷ Mr
Rearranges to: Mass (g) = moles × Mr  |  Mr = mass (g) ÷ moles
Atomic masses to use: H=1 | C=12 | N=14 | O=16 | Na=23 | Mg=24 | S=32 | Cl=35.5 | Ca=40 | Cu=64

✏️ Worked Example 1 – Calculating Mr FTHT

Question: Calculate the Mr of sulfuric acid, H₂SO₄.
Step 1: Write out each atom and its Ar: H = 1, S = 32, O = 16
Step 2: Count how many of each: 2×H + 1×S + 4×O
Step 3: Calculate: (2×1) + (1×32) + (4×16) = 2 + 32 + 64 = 98

✏️ Worked Example 2 – Moles from mass HT

Question: Calculate the number of moles in 11.7 g of NaCl.
Step 1: Mr of NaCl = 23 + 35.5 = 58.5
Step 2: Moles = mass ÷ Mr = 11.7 ÷ 58.5 = 0.2 mol

✏️ Worked Example 3 – Mass from moles HT

Question: Calculate the mass of 0.5 moles of calcium carbonate (CaCO₃).
Step 1: Mr of CaCO₃ = 40 + 12 + (3×16) = 40 + 12 + 48 = 100
Step 2: Mass = moles × Mr = 0.5 × 100 = 50 g

Week 5 – Acids, Bases & Neutralisation

Paper 1 | Topic 11 (Combined) | Both tiers | Required Practical

🧪 Key spec facts:
• Acids produce hydrogen ions (H⁺) in aqueous solution. pH < 7.
• Aqueous solutions of alkalis contain hydroxide ions (OH⁻). pH > 7. Neutral = pH 7.
• Neutralisation: H⁺ + OH⁻ → H₂O (the ionic equation for all acid-alkali neutralisations)
• Acid + base → salt + water  |  Acid + metal → salt + hydrogen
• Acid + metal carbonate → salt + water + carbon dioxide
• Salt name: first part from metal/base, second part from acid:
  – hydrochloric acid → chloride  |  sulfuric acid → sulfate  |  nitric acid → nitrate
• Titration uses a burette to add acid/alkali drop by drop to find exact volumes that react.

Week 6 – The Reactivity Series & Metal Extraction

Paper 1 | Topic 11 (Combined) | Both tiers

⚡ Key spec facts:
• The reactivity of a metal is related to its tendency to form positive ions.
• A more reactive metal can displace a less reactive metal from a compound.
• Oxidation = gain of oxygen (or loss of electrons, HT). Reduction = loss of oxygen (or gain of electrons, HT).
• Metals less reactive than carbon can be extracted by reduction with carbon.
• Metals more reactive than carbon (e.g. aluminium) must be extracted by electrolysis.
• HT: Oxidation is loss of electrons (OIL RIG: Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain).

⚡ The Correct Reactivity Series (AQA)

From most reactive (top) to least reactive (bottom). You must learn this order.

K Potassium — too reactive, extracted by electrolysis
Na Sodium — reacts vigorously with cold water
Li Lithium — reacts with cold water
Ca Calcium — reacts with cold water
Mg Magnesium — reacts very slowly with water, vigorous with acid
Al Aluminium — (for extraction Qs: more reactive than C → needs electrolysis)
C Carbon ← metals BELOW here extracted with carbon
Zn Zinc — reacts with dilute acid
Fe Iron — reacts slowly with dilute acid
H Hydrogen ← metals BELOW here don't react with acid
Cu Copper — does NOT react with dilute acid (least reactive on AQA list)
⚠️ AQA Combined Science spec requires this exact list: K, Na, Li, Ca, Mg, C, Zn, Fe, H, Cu — learn this order! Carbon sits between magnesium and zinc. Hydrogen sits between iron and copper. Metals above hydrogen react with dilute acid → produce H₂ gas. Metals below hydrogen (like copper) do not react with dilute acid. Aluminium is needed for extraction questions — it's more reactive than carbon, so it must be extracted by electrolysis.

📝 Exam-style Questions – Metal Extraction

(2 marks)(a) Explain why iron can be extracted from iron oxide by reduction with carbon, but aluminium cannot be extracted this way.

✅ Iron is less reactive than carbon ✓, so carbon can displace / reduce iron from its oxide. Aluminium is more reactive than carbon ✓, so carbon cannot reduce aluminium oxide — electrolysis must be used instead.

(2 marks)(b) A student places a piece of zinc into copper sulfate solution. The solution changes from blue to colourless and a brown/orange solid appears. Write the word equation and explain what has happened.

✅ zinc + copper sulfate → zinc sulfate + copper ✓

(3 marks)(c) In the displacement reaction Zn + CuSO₄ → ZnSO₄ + Cu, identify which substance is oxidised and which is reduced. Explain your answer in terms of electron transfer. HT

✅ Zinc is oxidised — it loses electrons (Zn → Zn²⁺ + 2e⁻) ✓
Copper is reduced — copper ions gain electrons (Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu) ✓
OIL RIG: Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain ✓

(2 marks)(d) A student wants to find out if metal X is more or less reactive than iron. She places a piece of metal X into iron sulfate solution. No reaction occurs. She then places a piece of iron into a solution of metal X sulfate. A reaction occurs. Place metal X in the reactivity series relative to iron and copper. Explain your reasoning.

Week 7 – Rates of Reaction & Required Practical

Paper 2 | Topic 13 (Combined) | Both tiers | Required Practical

🔥 Key spec facts — factors that increase rate of reaction:
1. ↑ Concentration (or pressure for gases) — more particles in the same volume → more frequent collisions
2. ↑ Temperature — particles move faster → more frequent collisions and more particles have energy ≥ activation energy
3. ↑ Surface area — more particles exposed to react → more frequent collisions
4. Add a catalyst — provides an alternative reaction pathway with a lower activation energy

Activation energy = the minimum amount of energy particles must have to react when they collide.
⚠️ Collision theory mark scheme tip: A full 3-mark collision theory answer must include: (1) particles have more energy / move faster, (2) collisions are more frequent, (3) more collisions have energy ≥ the activation energy (more successful collisions). AQA specifically require the phrase activation energy for the highest marks.

Week 8 – Electrolysis

Paper 1 | Topic 11 (Combined) | Both tiers | Required Practical

⚡ Key spec facts:
• Electrolysis uses an electric current to break down an ionic compound (the electrolyte).
• Electrolysis only works when the ionic compound is melted or dissolved in water — because the ions must be free to move.
• Positive ions move to the negative electrode (cathode) → metals or hydrogen are produced here.
• Negative ions move to the positive electrode (anode) → non-metals are produced here (e.g. oxygen or chlorine).
• For aqueous solutions: hydrogen is produced at the cathode if the metal is more reactive than hydrogen. Oxygen is produced at the anode unless the solution contains halide ions, in which case the halogen is produced.
• Electrolysis is used to extract reactive metals (e.g. aluminium from aluminium oxide/cryolite mixture) that cannot be extracted by reduction with carbon.
HT only: At the cathode, positive ions gain electrons → reduction. At the anode, negative ions lose electrons → oxidation. Half equations can be written for these reactions.

Week 9 – Organic Chemistry: Crude Oil & Hydrocarbons

Paper 2 | Topic 14 (Combined) | Both tiers

🛢️ Key spec facts:
• Crude oil is a mixture of hydrocarbons (compounds containing only carbon and hydrogen).
• Separated by fractional distillation: different fractions condense at different temperatures because they have different chain lengths → different strengths of intermolecular forces.
• Longer chains → stronger intermolecular forces → higher boiling point, more viscous (thicker), less flammable.
• Shorter chains → weaker intermolecular forces → lower boiling point, more flammable, less viscous.
• Alkanes have the general formula CₙH₂ₙ₊₂. They have single C–C bonds only (saturated).
• Alkenes have a C=C double bond (unsaturated). General formula CₙH₂ₙ.
• Cracking breaks down long-chain hydrocarbons into shorter, more useful molecules including alkenes.
• Test for an alkene: add bromine water — it decolourises (orange/brown → colourless) because the alkene reacts across the double bond. Alkanes do NOT decolourise bromine water.

🔬 RP1 – Preparation of a Pure, Dry Sample of a Soluble Salt (Links to W5 Acids)

Aim: Make pure crystals of a soluble salt (e.g. copper sulfate) from an insoluble base (e.g. copper oxide) and an acid (e.g. sulfuric acid).

Method:
1. Warm the dilute acid gently in a beaker.
2. Add the insoluble base (e.g. copper oxide) a spatula at a time, stirring after each addition.
3. Continue adding until the base is in excess — you know it's in excess when some unreacted solid remains at the bottom.
4. Filter the mixture to remove the excess solid. The filtrate contains the dissolved salt.
5. Pour the filtrate into an evaporating basin and heat gently (or leave to crystallise slowly) until crystals form.
6. Pat dry with filter paper.

Why add excess base? To make sure all the acid has reacted — so the salt solution is not contaminated with unreacted acid.

(1 mark)(a) Why is the acid warmed before adding the copper oxide?

To increase the rate of reaction
To melt the copper oxide
To evaporate the water

(2 marks)(b) How does the student know when enough copper oxide has been added?

✅ When the copper oxide is in excess / when unreacted copper oxide remains at the bottom of the beaker after stirring ✓. This shows all the acid has been used up ✓.

(2 marks)(c) Name the two separation techniques used in this practical and explain what each one removes.

✅ Filtration — removes the excess (unreacted) copper oxide solid ✓.
Evaporation / crystallisation — removes the water to leave pure dry copper sulfate crystals ✓.

🔬 RP2 – Electrolysis of Aqueous Solutions (Links to W8 Electrolysis)

Aim: Investigate what is produced during the electrolysis of aqueous solutions using inert (carbon/graphite) electrodes.

Method:
1. Pour the electrolyte solution (e.g. copper sulfate, sodium chloride) into a beaker.
2. Place two inert electrodes (graphite or platinum) into the solution, connected to a DC power supply.
3. Turn on the power supply and observe what happens at each electrode.
4. Test any gases produced (e.g. squeaky pop for hydrogen, relights a glowing splint for oxygen, bleaches damp litmus paper for chlorine).

Key results:
• Copper sulfate solution → copper deposited at cathode (brown coating), oxygen at anode (bubbles).
• Sodium chloride solution (brine) → hydrogen at cathode (bubbles, squeaky pop), chlorine at anode (bubbles, bleaches litmus).

(1 mark)(a) Why must the electrodes be inert (e.g. graphite)?

✅ So the electrodes do not react with the electrolyte or the products ✓. This ensures only the ions in the solution are involved in the reaction.

(3 marks)(b) A student electrolyses copper sulfate solution. Describe what they would observe at each electrode and explain why copper forms at the cathode.

✅ At the cathode: a brown/orange coating of copper forms ✓. At the anode: bubbles of gas (oxygen) are produced ✓. Copper forms at the cathode because copper is less reactive than hydrogen, so copper ions are discharged / reduced at the cathode instead of hydrogen ions ✓.

(2 marks)(c) Describe the test for chlorine gas and state the result.

✅ Hold damp (blue) litmus paper near the gas ✓. Chlorine bleaches the litmus paper white / decolourises it ✓.

🔬 RP3 – Rates of Reaction: Effect of Concentration (Links to W7 Rates)

Aim: Investigate how changing the concentration of sodium thiosulfate affects the rate of reaction with hydrochloric acid.

Method:
1. Draw a black cross on white paper. Place a conical flask on top of it.
2. Measure a set volume of sodium thiosulfate solution at a known concentration. Add to the flask.
3. Add a set volume of dilute hydrochloric acid and start the timer immediately.
4. Look down through the flask from above. Stop the timer when the cross can no longer be seen (sulfur precipitate makes the solution cloudy).
5. Repeat with different concentrations of sodium thiosulfate (dilute with water, keeping total volume the same).

Independent variable: concentration of sodium thiosulfate
Dependent variable: time for the cross to disappear
Control variables: volume of solutions, concentration of HCl, temperature, same cross

(1 mark)(a) Why is this experiment considered to have a subjective end point?

✅ Different people may judge the point at which the cross disappears differently ✓. This makes the end point subjective / not precise / open to human error.

(2 marks)(b) The student dilutes the sodium thiosulfate with water for each experiment. Why must the total volume of liquid always stay the same?

✅ To keep it a fair test ✓. If the volume changed, the depth of liquid in the flask would change, which would affect how quickly the cross disappears — it would no longer be testing only the effect of concentration ✓.

(2 marks)(c) The student's results show: 40 g/dm³ → 92 s, 30 g/dm³ → 128 s, 20 g/dm³ → 195 s, 10 g/dm³ → 410 s. Using collision theory, explain why a higher concentration leads to a faster reaction.

✅ Higher concentration means more particles of sodium thiosulfate in the same volume ✓. This leads to more frequent collisions between reactant particles, so more successful collisions per second / a higher rate of reaction ✓.

🔬 RP4 – Temperature Changes in Reacting Solutions (Exothermic & Endothermic)

Aim: Investigate the temperature change when an acid reacts with an alkali or a metal.

Method:
1. Use a polystyrene cup as an insulating container (reduces heat loss).
2. Measure a set volume of acid and record the starting temperature.
3. Add the alkali (or metal powder) and stir with the thermometer.
4. Record the highest (or lowest) temperature reached.
5. Calculate temperature change = final temp − starting temp.

Key terms:
• Exothermic = temperature increases → energy is released to the surroundings (e.g. neutralisation, combustion).
• Endothermic = temperature decreases → energy is taken in from the surroundings (e.g. citric acid + sodium hydrogencarbonate, thermal decomposition).

(1 mark)(a) Why is a polystyrene cup used instead of a glass beaker?

Glass would break if the reaction is vigorous
Polystyrene is an insulator — it reduces heat loss to the surroundings
Polystyrene is easier to read the temperature through

(2 marks)(b) A student adds magnesium to dilute hydrochloric acid in a polystyrene cup. The temperature rises from 21°C to 34°C. Is this reaction exothermic or endothermic? Explain your answer.

✅ Exothermic ✓ because the temperature increased, which means energy was released / transferred to the surroundings ✓.

(2 marks)(c) Suggest two ways the student could improve the accuracy of the temperature change measured.

✅ Any two from: Use a lid on the polystyrene cup (reduces heat loss) ✓ | Use a more precise thermometer / datalogger (e.g. reads to 0.1°C) ✓ | Repeat the experiment and calculate a mean ✓ | Ensure the solutions are at the same starting temperature ✓

🔬 RP5 – Chromatography (Paper 2 — Separating & Identifying Substances)

Aim: Use paper chromatography to separate and identify substances in a mixture (e.g. food colourings or inks).

Method:
1. Draw a pencil line (baseline) near the bottom of chromatography paper (pencil because it is insoluble and won't dissolve in the solvent).
2. Place small spots of the mixtures and known reference dyes on the baseline.
3. Lower the paper into the solvent — the solvent level must be below the baseline (otherwise the spots dissolve into the solvent).
4. Allow the solvent to travel up the paper. Remove when the solvent is near the top.
5. Mark the solvent front with a pencil line.
6. Substances are identified by comparing the distance they travel to the reference spots, or by calculating Rf values.
📐 Rf = distance moved by substance ÷ distance moved by solvent
Rf values are always between 0 and 1 and have no units.

✏️ Worked Example – Rf Calculation

Question: A spot of dye travels 3.6 cm. The solvent front travels 9.0 cm. Calculate the Rf value.
Step 1: Rf = distance moved by substance ÷ distance moved by solvent
Step 2: Rf = 3.6 ÷ 9.0 = 0.40

(1 mark)(a) Why must the baseline be drawn in pencil, not ink?

Pencil is easier to see
Pencil is insoluble and won't dissolve in the solvent / won't move up the paper
Pencil is darker than ink

(1 mark)(b) A substance produces only one spot on the chromatogram. What does this tell you?

The substance is a pure substance (only one component)
The substance is a mixture of two dyes

(2 marks)(c) A spot of dye X travels 4.2 cm and the solvent front travels 7.0 cm. Calculate the Rf value. A reference table shows Dye A has Rf = 0.40 and Dye B has Rf = 0.60. Identify dye X.

✅ Rf = 4.2 ÷ 7.0 = 0.60 ✓
Dye X is Dye B (matching Rf = 0.60) ✓
